## Tip 1: Expo Experience & Bare Workflow Are Two Separate Skillsets

Most React Native developers learned through Expo's managed workflow. That works for MVPs with standard features. It breaks down when your product needs custom native integrations, Bluetooth, biometrics, or background processing. None of these is supported by the Expo managed workflow.

Bare workflow developers write native code, understand the JavaScript-to-native bridge, and debug across the platform layer. Expo-only developers cannot do this without a substantial ramp-up. Bare developers can always drop into an Expo environment; the reverse is rarely true.

**Ask this**: "Walk me through a time when you had to eject from Expo or work outside the managed workflow. What broke, and how did you fix it?"

A candidate who has *only done Expo projects* will not have a confident answer.

## Tip 3: App Store Links Are the First Filter, Not the Last

One of the most direct tips to [hire React Native developers](https://www.wildnetedge.com/mobile-app-development/react-native-app-development): request live App Store or Google Play links before scheduling any call.

Shipping a production app means navigating store submission, handling rejection cycles, managing versioning, and keeping an app stable through OS updates. None of this can be *faked* in a tutorial.

- Ask for apps they built or contributed to directly.
- Install the app & test transitions, offline behavior, and cold start performance.
- Check the last update date. An app abandoned two-plus years ago signals low long-term ownership.

**Ask this**: "What was your most recent App Store or Play Store rejection, and how did you resolve it?"

## Tip 4: Native Module Experience Costs 20% More, But Prevents Rewrites

Developers who write custom Swift and Kotlin bridges earn a 15 to 20 percent salary premium above standard rates. Usually, the premium is skipped when the budget is tight.

But when your app needs device-level integrations like NFC, advanced camera controls, or biometric APIs, a developer without experience in native modules is a bad choice. The workaround is usually a costly rewrite or months of delays.

**Example**: A fintech team hired mid-level React Native developers to cut costs. When a biometric login required Kotlin-level code, none of them could build it. The fix costs more than the salary of hiring a premium developer for the whole thing.

## Tip 6: React.js on Resume ≠ React Native Experience

Many hiring managers see "React" and assume mobile competence. **React.js developers** build web interfaces with HTML and CSS. **React Native developers** build mobile apps with native components, handle platform-specific rendering, and manage App Store & Play Store submissions.

These are different roles. A senior React.js developer cannot *substitute* a senior React Native developer without significant help.

**Ask this**: "How do you handle rendering differences between iOS and Android in the same codebase?"

A web-first developer will not have a concrete & practical answer.

## Tip 7: State Management Choice Reveals Architectural Thinking Better Than Any Test

Ask a candidate to explain why they chose Redux, Zustand, Context API, or MobX for a specific project. An **experienced developer** will describe trade-offs grounded in team size, data complexity, and scale. A less experienced one will say, "I always use Redux."

This is one of the most overlooked tips to hire React Native developers: architectural judgment that does not show up on a resume or in algorithm questions.

**Ask**: "What would you change about the state management in your last production app, and why?"

Thoughtful critiques signal mature engineering judgment. Defensive answers signal someone who learned patterns without understanding them.

## Tip 8: For Remote Hires, Async Communication Is a Technical Skill

To hire remote React Native developers well, evaluate async communication before the technical screen, not after.

**Test**: Send a written prompt asking the candidate to explain a trade-off they made in a recent project. **Evaluate**:

- Is the answer structured and specific, or vague and padded?
- Does it demonstrate engineering judgment without prompting?
- Is it appropriately concise?

Developers who cannot communicate clearly in writing create friction across every PR review, sprint update, and architecture discussion. For cross platform developers in the USA hiring remote teams, this test catches more mismatches than any other.

## Tip 9: Offshore Rates Are 40 to 60% Lower & the Quality Gap Is a Myth (Mostly)

Eastern European and Latin American developers deliver React Native work at just 40 to 60 percent of US rates. Senior developers in Poland, Romania, Brazil, and Argentina typically earn $65,000 to $100,000 annually, compared with $140,000 to $185,000 in the USA.

Timezone works in your favor: Eastern Europe overlaps US mornings, and Latin America overlaps most of the US workday.

The quality myth persists because poorly executed offshore hiring looks like an offshore problem. It is always a *scoping problem*. A clear technical spec covering Expo vs. Bare, must-have skills, and platform scope resolves the majority of quality issues regardless of geography.

## Tip 10: Choose Dedicated Over Freelance, For Projects Over 90 Days

**Freelancers** context-switch between clients. Every time an engagement resumes after a gap, the onboarding cost is paid again. Institutional knowledge covering edge cases, product decisions, and architecture history does not transfer reliably in a handoff document.

**Dedicated developers** retain context across the full project lifecycle. They flag regressions before shipping the code, and it accelerates as the codebase matures. For React Native mobile app services spanning multiple quarters, the economics of dedicated developers are unbeatable.

**Pro Tip**: Calculate re-onboarding cost *before signing a freelance contract* for anything longer than 90 days. It is always higher than the rate difference.
